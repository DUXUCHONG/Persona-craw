## Life Assistant

### Before: The Groundhog Day Problem

You've been using AI assistants for a while. They're helpful — sometimes.

But every conversation starts from scratch.

Monday: "Plan me a trip to Tokyo. I like boutique hotels, not chains."

The AI plans a nice trip. Boutique hotels. Great.

Thursday: "Plan me a weekend getaway to Kyoto."

It recommends a Hilton.

You stare at the screen. You *just* told it you hate chains. Three days ago. But it doesn't remember. It never remembers.

You try again: "I said I prefer boutique hotels."

It fixes it. This time. Until next time.

It's like having a coworker with amnesia. Every morning, you re-introduce yourself.

You write a "preferences" document and paste it into every conversation. It sort of works. But you're doing the assistant's job — managing its memory for it.

This is the life of every AI user in 2025. The AI is smart in the moment. But it never learns.

---

### After: Persona-craw, Week 1

You switch to Persona-craw. It looks the same. You talk to it the same way.

> "Plan a trip to Tokyo. Boutique hotels, walking-distance restaurants."

Standard result. Good, not amazing.

You also ask it to draft an email to a friend.

> "Too formal. I talk to my friends casually — like texting."

It rewrites. Better. You send it.

Nothing special yet. Feels like any other assistant.

### Week 3

You're planning Paris now. You didn't mention hotels.

The itinerary comes back: boutique hotel in Le Marais, three restaurants within walking distance.

You didn't ask.

You smile a little. *It remembered.*

You also started using it for meal planning.

> "Nah, skip the sushi. More salads."

By the next week, meal plans are salad-heavy. The week after that, you don't even mention it. It just happens.

The emails it drafts sound casual now. Like you. You haven't corrected tone in two weeks.

### Month 2

Something has shifted. You don't explain things anymore.

> "Remind me about the gym."

Monday and Thursday. You mentioned this once, five weeks ago.

Shopping lists look like something you'd write. The assistant suggests items you actually buy — not generic recommendations.

A complicated task comes up: multi-city trip, connecting flights, visa requirements, tight budget. You type it in and wait.

A few seconds longer than usual. Behind the scenes, a powerful model worked out the logistics. What you see is a clean plan: boutique hotels, walkable restaurants, casual formatting. Your style, applied automatically.

Next time you plan something similar, it's instant. Your local model absorbed the pattern.

### Month 6

You don't think about the assistant anymore. It's just part of how you live.

Meal plans are right. Emails sound like you. Gym reminders appear at the right time. Travel matches your taste without asking.

One day, your friend asks: "How do you get your AI to know your stuff so well?"

You think about it. You never configured anything. You never uploaded a preference file. You never pressed a "train" button.

You just... talked to it. And it learned.

Every correction you made, every casual remark, every "nah, not like that" — it turned all of it into something permanent. Your words became its skills.

The amnesia problem is gone. This one remembers.

## Enterprise Assistant

### Before: The Eternal Intern

You've had AI assistants at work. They're fast at generating text. But they never learn your workflow.

Every Monday, you write a report. Every Monday, the AI generates something wrong.

> "Wrong format. I told you — use the team template."

You paste the template. Again. For the fourth week in a row.

You ask it to scaffold a Python project.

> "Too many files. I always keep things flat."

> "Pytest, not unittest."

It fixes it. This time. Next project, same mistakes.

You ask it to draft a client email.

> "Too corporate. I'm warm but professional. Like my last email."

It doesn't know what your last email sounded like. It has no memory of it.

You start keeping a running document of "rules for the AI." You paste it at the top of every conversation. The document is three pages long and growing.

You are the AI's memory manager. That's not what you signed up for.

### After: Persona-craw, Day 1

First task: weekly report.

It generates something generic. Wrong format.

> "Wrong format. Use our team's report template. Metrics table up front. Skip the intro."

You paste the template. It rewrites. Okay, not bad.

Nothing special yet. Same old.

### Week 2

New Python project.

> "Too many files. Keep it flat until it grows."

> "Always pytest. Never unittest."

> "No Docker for small projects."

Client email draft arrives:

> "Too stiff. Match my last few emails — polished but not corporate."

It adjusts. Closer, not perfect.

### Week 4

Monday. Report time.

The report comes back: team template. Metrics table first. No intro. Correct formatting.

You didn't remind it.

You scaffold another project. Flat structure. Pytest. No Docker.

You didn't mention any of it.

The client email reads like you wrote it. Warm, polished, not stiff.

You sit back. Something changed. You didn't paste any "rules document." You didn't set up a prompt template. It just... absorbed your corrections.

### Month 2

A hard problem shows up: system architecture for a new microservice. You're not sure about the approach.

You ask Persona-craw. It takes a moment.

Behind the scenes, a powerful model explored three approaches, evaluated trade-offs, identified failure modes. What you see is a clear recommendation with rationale.

A week later, a similar question comes up. This time the answer is instant. Your daily model learned the pattern.

You also notice something subtle: it's not just remembering individual rules anymore. Your corrections from different weeks have merged into compound skills. Flat project structure + pytest + minimal CI + your doc format + your naming conventions — all combined into a unified engineering profile that it applies to everything.

### Month 4

> "Focus on experiments in paper summaries. Skip background. Compare to what I read last week."

Done. Every summary from now on.

> "The quarterly review — same structure as last quarter, just update the competitive analysis section."

It pulls the structure, updates exactly that section, leaves everything else consistent.

Complex data analysis lands on your desk. Your local model handles the routine parts — formatting, basic stats, your preferred chart style. When it gets complicated, the cloud model takes over. You never see the handoff. The results arrive in your format.

### Month 8

A new team member joins. They ask how you like reports done.

You almost say: "Just ask my AI."

Reports, emails, code scaffolds, data analysis, paper summaries — everything comes out in your style, your format, your voice. The AI carries your complete professional profile.

When something new appears — a type of task it hasn't seen — the powerful model figures it out. Next time, your daily model knows how.

You update a preference occasionally — "switching from pip to uv" — and within a day, every project reflects the change. The old skill is gone. Replaced.

You used to manage the AI's memory. Now the AI manages its own skills. Built from your words, refined from your reactions, updated when you change your mind.

The eternal intern grew up. It became a working partner.
